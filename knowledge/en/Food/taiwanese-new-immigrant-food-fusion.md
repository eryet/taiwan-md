# Taiwanese New Immigrant Food Fusion

> "The flavors of home never disappear; they bloom into different flowers on new soil."
> 
> —— Nguyen Thi Van (Taiwanese-Vietnamese housewife, 20 years in Taiwan)

In the underground streets of Taipei Main Station, an inconspicuous small shop emits the aroma of lemongrass and coconut milk. The shop owner, Aga from Indonesia, uses local Taiwanese ingredients to create authentic Indonesian curry. "Taiwan's cabbage is sweeter than Indonesia's," she says, "so my curry has a milder taste that Taiwanese people prefer more."

This small adjustment symbolizes the core characteristic of Taiwan's new immigrant food culture—not simple replication, but creative fusion. Since the 1990s, new immigrants from various Southeast Asian countries have gradually arrived in Taiwan, bringing not only labor but also rich and colorful food cultures. Today, these foreign flavors have deeply integrated into Taiwan's culinary landscape, becoming the most delicious testimony to Taiwan's multicultural society.

## Development Trajectory of New Immigrant Cuisine in Taiwan

### 1990-2000: Beginning of Taking Root

In the 1990s, Taiwan began seeing large numbers of transnational marriages, with women from Vietnam, Indonesia, Thailand, the Philippines and other countries gradually arriving. Initially, they mainly cooked hometown dishes at home for their families, with these foreign flavors circulating only within small circles.

During this period, new immigrant cuisine maintained relatively pure original forms. Vietnamese pho (rice noodles), Thai Tom Yam (sour-spicy soup), Indonesian Rendang (rendang curry) all tried to maintain their homeland's original flavors. However, due to difficulties obtaining ingredients, many new immigrants began learning to substitute Taiwanese local ingredients for their homeland spices and vegetables.

### 2000-2010: Commercial Sprouting

Entering the 21st century, as the number of new immigrants increased and Taiwanese society's acceptance of multicultural elements grew, new immigrant cuisine began moving from households into commercial markets.

Vietnamese pho stalls began appearing in Taipei's Huaxi Street and Nanjianchang Night Market; Zhongzheng New Village in Zhongli, which gathered many ethnic Chinese immigrants from Thailand and Myanmar, formed a strong Yunnan Thai-Myanmar flavor zone; the area around Taoyuan Train Station became a gathering place for Indonesian cuisine.

During this period, new immigrant cuisine began showing trends of "localization." To adapt to Taiwanese taste preferences, many dishes adjusted their spiciness, sourness, and sweetness levels. Thai cuisine reduced fish sauce usage and increased soy sauce proportions; Vietnamese cuisine decreased cilantro amounts and added chives familiar to Taiwanese people.

### 2010-2020: Mainstreaming and Refinement

In the 2010s, new immigrant cuisine welcomed rapid development. Taiwanese society's interest in Southeast Asian culture grew daily, and with internet media promotion, new immigrant cuisine began entering mainstream consumer markets.

Many famous new immigrant restaurant brands emerged during this period. Viet Pho made Vietnamese pho into chain operations; Wowprime Corporation, although founded by Taiwanese, hired many Thai chefs to promote authentic Thai cuisine; Coconut Thai specialized in Malaysian and Singaporean cuisines.

Meanwhile, new immigrant cuisine also began showing refinement trends. Some new immigrant chefs began emphasizing ingredient quality and plating aesthetics, elevating traditional street food to refined restaurant cuisine.

### 2020-Present: Fusion Innovation and Cultural Identity

In recent years, Taiwan's new immigrant cuisine has developed more diverse and innovative aspects. Second-generation new immigrants (children of new immigrants) began participating in culinary culture inheritance and innovation, interpreting their parents' homeland cuisines in more creative ways.

"Taiwan-style Vietnamese pho," "Thai-style braised pork rice," "Indonesian-style fried chicken" and other fusion dishes began appearing. These innovations were no longer seen as betrayals of tradition but as natural results of cultural fusion.

## Major Types and Characteristics of New Immigrant Cuisine

### Vietnamese Cuisine: Fresh and Sweet-Sour Southeast Asian Style

Vietnamese cuisine is one of Taiwan's most widespread new immigrant cuisines. Its characteristics include fresh flavors, balanced sweet-sour tastes, and extensive use of fresh herbs.

**Pho (rice noodles)** represents Vietnamese cuisine. In Taiwan, Vietnamese pho underwent significant localization modifications:

- **Broth adjustments**: Originally light beef bone broth often has more spices added in Taiwan for richer flavors
- **Side dish localization**: Bean sprouts, chives and other easily available Taiwanese vegetables replace some original Vietnamese herbs
- **Meat choices**: Added pork options preferred by Taiwanese, not limited to traditional beef

**Vietnamese spring rolls** are also quite popular in Taiwan. Taiwanese versions of Vietnamese spring rolls typically:

- Increase proportions of lettuce and cucumber
- Use local Taiwanese shrimp and pork shreds
- Adjust dipping sauce to milder sweet-sour flavors

**Representative establishments**:
- A-He Vietnamese Pho (Taipei): Opened by Vietnamese new immigrant A-He, insists on using Vietnamese imported spices
- Mei Feng Vietnamese Restaurant (Zhongli): Combines Vietnamese and Taiwanese flavors, deeply loved by locals

### Thai Cuisine: Aromatic and Spicy Tropical Flavors

Thai cuisine is famous for its aromatic spiciness, gradually adapting to Taiwanese taste preferences during development in Taiwan.

**Tom Yam (Thai sour-spicy soup)** is one of the most popular Thai dishes:

- **Sourness adjustment**: Reduced lime leaves usage, increased lemon juice for gentler sourness
- **Spiciness control**: Offers different spice levels to meet Taiwanese people's varying spice tolerance
- **Seafood localization**: Uses local Taiwanese shrimp and squid for higher freshness

**Green curry** also underwent innovative modifications in Taiwan:

- Uses local Taiwanese eggplant and string beans
- Adjusts coconut milk concentration to suit Taiwanese taste preferences
- Served with Taiwanese rice rather than traditional Thai fragrant rice

**Pad Krapow Moo** (Thai stir-fried pork) is a popular street food choice:

- Uses Taiwanese warm pork for more tender meat
- Nine-story pagoda (Thai basil) partially replaces Thai basil for Taiwanese spice preferences
- Side dishes include Taiwanese pickled vegetables and braised eggs

**Representative establishments**:
- Thai Flavor Restaurant (Taipei): Opened by Thai new immigrants, authentic flavors
- Zhongzheng Market (Taoyuan): Gathers multiple Thai-Myanmar food stalls

### Indonesian Cuisine: Spice-Rich Archipelago Flavors

Indonesian cuisine features rich spices and complex flavor layers. In Taiwan, Indonesian cuisine development was relatively late but has gradually gained attention in recent years.

**Nasi Goreng (Indonesian fried rice)** is the most easily accepted Indonesian dish by Taiwanese:

- Uses local Taiwanese long-grain rice for textures more familiar to Taiwanese
- Seasoning leans sweet-salty with mild spiciness
- Adds local ingredients like Taiwanese sausage and braised eggs

**Rendang curry** after Taiwanese modifications:

- Adjusts coconut milk concentration to avoid being too heavy
- Reduces spice amounts to suit Taiwanese tastes
- Served with Taiwanese white rice rather than traditional coconut rice

**Representative establishments**:
- Bali Theme Restaurant: Though run by Taiwanese, employs Indonesian chefs for quite authentic cuisine
- Indonesia Street (Taipei Main Station underground): Gathers multiple Indonesian cuisine shops

### Filipino Cuisine: Sweet-Sour and Appetizing Island Country Flavors

Filipino cuisine development in Taiwan is relatively niche but still has its unique charm.

**Adobo (Filipino braised meat)** is the Philippines' national dish:

- Uses Taiwanese soy sauce and vinegar for milder flavors
- Adds radish and potatoes beloved by Taiwanese
- Served with Taiwanese white rice

**Representative establishments**:
- Filipino House (Taipei): Family-style restaurant opened by Filipino new immigrants

## Localization Innovations in New Immigrant Cuisine

### Localization of Ingredient Substitutions

The first challenge new immigrant chefs faced in Taiwan was ingredient procurement. Many homeland spices and vegetables were not easily available in Taiwan or were expensive. Therefore, they showed amazing creativity, using local Taiwanese ingredients to create new flavor combinations.

**Spice substitutions**:
- Use Taiwanese pepper powder to replace some Southeast Asian specialty spices
- Use Taiwanese cilantro to replace Vietnamese cilantro (slightly different flavors)
- Use Taiwanese chilies to replace Thai bird's eye chilies

**Vegetable substitutions**:
- Use Taiwanese lettuce to replace Vietnamese lettuce
- Use Taiwanese chives to replace Thai chive flowers
- Use Taiwanese eggplant to replace Thai round eggplant

**Meat adjustments**:
- Use Taiwan's high-quality pork and chicken
- Adjust meat cut selections based on Taiwanese preferences

### Taiwanese-Style Taste Adjustments

To adapt to Taiwanese taste preferences, new immigrant cuisines adjusted spiciness, sourness, and sweetness levels.

**Spice moderation**:
Taiwanese people generally cannot handle spice as well as Southeast Asians, so many new immigrant dishes reduced spiciness or offered different spice level choices.

**Increased sweetness**:
Taiwanese people prefer sweet flavors, so many new immigrant dishes increased sweetness. Thai stir-fried noodles add more sugar; Vietnamese pho broth also has slight sweetness.

**Salt adjustments**:
Reduced usage of fish sauce and other strongly seafood-flavored seasonings, increased proportions of soy sauce familiar to Taiwanese.

### Fusion of Cooking Techniques

New immigrant chefs also incorporated Taiwanese cooking techniques into their cuisine.

**Stir-frying techniques**:
Many Southeast Asian dishes originally required long stewing, but in Taiwan's fast-paced life, new immigrant chefs learned Taiwanese stir-frying methods to shorten cooking times.

**Braising techniques**:
Taiwan's braising techniques were applied to Southeast Asian cuisine, producing innovative dishes like "Thai-style braised meat" and "Vietnamese-style braised eggs."

**Frying improvements**:
Learning Taiwan night market frying techniques to make Southeast Asian fried foods crispier.

## Cross-Cultural Fusion Innovative Cuisines

### Taiwan-Vietnam Fusion Dishes

**Vietnamese-style braised pork rice**:
Combines Taiwan's classic braised pork rice with Vietnamese spices, using lemongrass and mint herbs for seasoning to create a refreshing version of braised pork rice.

**Vietnamese-style beef noodles**:
Cooks Taiwanese beef noodles using Vietnamese pho's clear soup techniques for more refreshing broth, adding Vietnamese cilantro and bean sprouts.

### Taiwan-Thai Fusion Dishes

**Thai-style fried chicken**:
Combines Taiwan night market fried chicken with Thai seasonings, marinating with lemongrass and lime leaves.

**Thai-style gua bao**:
Changes traditional Taiwanese gua bao fillings to Thai-style grilled pork, paired with Thai-style papaya salad.

### Taiwan-Indonesian Fusion Dishes

**Indonesian-flavored stinky tofu**:
Seasons Taiwanese stinky tofu with Indonesian spices, creating unique "spiced stinky tofu."

**Curry fried chicken**:
Taiwanese fried chicken topped with Indonesian-style curry sauce, an East-meets-West creative dish.

## Social and Cultural Impact of New Immigrant Cuisine

### Changing Taiwanese Eating Habits

The popularization of new immigrant cuisine gradually changed Taiwanese eating habits and taste preferences.

**Increased spice acceptance**:
More and more Taiwanese began accepting and loving Southeast Asian spices like herbs, lemongrass, and lime leaves.

**Popularization of sour-spicy flavors**:
Sour-spicy dishes like Thai sour-spicy soup and Vietnamese sour-spicy pho became new favorites for many Taiwanese.

**Healthy eating concepts**:
Southeast Asian cuisine's extensive use of fresh vegetables and herbs promoted Taiwanese attention to healthy eating.

### Promoting Multicultural Understanding

New immigrant cuisine became an important window for Taiwanese society to understand Southeast Asian culture.

**Cultural exchange**:
Through tasting new immigrant cuisine, Taiwanese people gained deeper understanding and recognition of Southeast Asian culture.

**Eliminating prejudices**:
The charm of food helped eliminate some cultural prejudices, promoting understanding and integration between different ethnic groups.

**Language learning**:
Many Taiwanese began learning simple Vietnamese, Thai, and other languages because they loved new immigrant cuisine.

### Economic Impact

The development of new immigrant cuisine industry also brought important economic impacts.

**Employment opportunities**:
New immigrant cuisine restaurants provided important employment opportunities for new immigrants and cultivated related industry talent.

**Tourism benefits**:
Unique new immigrant cuisine became new highlights of Taiwan tourism, attracting international tourists to taste.

**Import-export trade**:
Demand for new immigrant cuisine drove import trade of Southeast Asian ingredients and seasonings.

## Cultural Inheritance and Innovation by Second-Generation New Immigrants

### Complexity of Cultural Identity

Second-generation new immigrants (children of new immigrants) face complex choices in cultural identity. They are both Taiwanese and inherit their mothers' homeland cultural bloodlines. In food culture, this complex identity recognition spawned many innovations.

**Language advantages**:
Second-generation new immigrants often speak fluent Chinese and their mothers' native languages, having unique communication advantages when running new immigrant restaurants.

**Creative thinking**:
Growing up in multicultural environments, second-generation new immigrants often have more open thinking in culinary innovation.

### New Generation of Innovative Cuisine

**Ms. Lin's Vietnam-Taiwan fusion restaurant**:
Ms. Lin, daughter of a Vietnamese new immigrant, opened a Vietnam-Taiwan fusion restaurant in Taipei. She combined Taiwan night market culture with Vietnamese street food, creating innovative dishes like "Vietnamese pepper buns" and "Taiwan-style spring rolls."

**Ah-Ming's Thai breakfast shop**:
Ah-Ming, son of a Thai new immigrant, combined Thai cuisine with Taiwan breakfast culture, launching fusion breakfasts like "Thai egg crepes" and "lemongrass soy milk."

### Promotion of Cultural Education

Many second-generation new immigrants began taking responsibility for cultural education, promoting Southeast Asian food culture through cooking classes and cultural activities.

**Cooking instruction**:
Offering Southeast Asian cooking courses, teaching Taiwanese to make authentic Southeast Asian cuisine.

**Cultural activities**:
Participating in school and community multicultural activities, promoting mothers' homeland culture through food displays.

## Challenges Faced and Future Prospects

### Major Challenges

**Ingredient supply chains**:
Many Southeast Asian specialty ingredients still rely on imports, with higher costs affecting cuisine price competitiveness.

**Skill inheritance**:
Some traditional preparation techniques face risks of being lost, requiring systematic inheritance mechanisms.

**Market acceptance**:
Although new immigrant cuisine is already widely popular, acceptance in certain regions or age groups still needs improvement.

**Commercial pressures**:
Over-changing traditional flavors to cater to market demands may lose original cultural characteristics.

### Development Opportunities

**Policy support**:
Government support policies for multiculturalism provide good environments for new immigrant cuisine development.

**High acceptance among younger generations**:
Younger generations have higher acceptance of foreign cuisine, providing broad market space for new immigrant cuisine.

**Tourism industry integration**:
New immigrant cuisine can deeply integrate with Taiwan's tourism industry, becoming important content for cultural tourism.

**Internationalization trends**:
With globalization development, Taiwan as a Southeast Asian food fusion base has potential for export to other countries.

### Future Prospects

**Professional development**:
More professional new immigrant cuisine restaurants will emerge, providing more refined services and more authentic flavors.

**Chain operations**:
Some successful new immigrant restaurant brands will develop chain operations, expanding market influence.

**Ingredient localization**:
As demand increases, some Southeast Asian vegetables and spices will be grown locally in Taiwan, reducing costs.

**Cultural education functions**:
New immigrant restaurants will take on more cultural education functions, becoming important venues for multicultural exchange.

**Innovative fusion cuisine**:
Second-generation new immigrants will create more Southeast Asian fusion dishes with Taiwan characteristics, forming unique "Taiwan-flavored Southeast Asian cuisine."

## Conclusion: Cultural Fusion of Taste

The development of Taiwan's new immigrant cuisine is a moving story about cultural fusion and innovation. It tells us that cultural inheritance is not static copying, but creative adaptation in new environments.

When Vietnamese aunties use Taiwanese cabbage to wrap sweeter spring rolls, when Thai daughters-in-law use Taiwanese pork to make milder pad krapow, when Indonesian sisters use Taiwanese chilies to adjust curry more suitable for locals, they are not betraying homeland traditions but creating new traditions belonging to Taiwan.

This fusion is bidirectional. While Taiwanese people accept new immigrant cuisine, they are also changing their own food culture. Today's Taiwanese cuisine is no longer the same as 20 years ago. It has become more diverse, richer, and more international.

The development of new immigrant cuisine in Taiwan demonstrates Taiwanese society's inclusiveness and creativity. It proves that exchanges between different cultures are not zero-sum games but can create "1+1>2" beautiful results.

In the future, with more second-generation new immigrants growing up and more innovative dishes appearing, Taiwan's new immigrant food culture will continue developing and evolving. It will become an important symbol of Taiwan's multiculturalism and add more colors and possibilities to Taiwan's food culture.

In this globalized era, the story of Taiwan's new immigrant cuisine also provides successful examples of cultural fusion for other countries. It tells us that open attitudes and innovative spirits can make different cultures bloom even more beautiful flowers on new soil.
