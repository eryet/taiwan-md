# History of Taiwanese Literature

> **30-second overview:** An island once asked "Does Taiwan have literature?" actually possesses a 400-year symphony of multiple voices. From indigenous oral epics to Lai He's vernacular Chinese revolution, from Pai Hsien-yung's "Taipei People" to contemporary digital creation, each shift in linguistic hegemony has catalyzed richer literary expressions. The 1987 publication of Yeh Shih-tao's "Outline of Taiwan Literary History" finally legitimized the island's literary tradition.

In 1945, after the Republic of China government took over Taiwan, a harsh question echoed through literary circles: "**Does Taiwan have literature?**" The arrogance behind this question overlooked a startling fact—while colonial authorities posed their doubts, this island had already nurtured four centuries of literary tradition. From Atayal creation myths to the New Literature Movement during Japanese rule, from Qing-era bamboo branch poetry to contemporary internet writing, Taiwan's literary history is an epic about voices seeking written form, words seeking home, and homeland seeking identity.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> The uniqueness of Taiwanese literature lies not in the continuation of a single tradition, but in innovative fusion after multiple cultural collisions. Each political transition and linguistic shift never eliminated literary traditions but instead catalyzed richer forms of expression.

## Indigenous Oral Literature: The First Voices (Prehistory–1624)

Before Chinese characters arrived in Taiwan, poetry already resonated here. Taiwan's earliest literary forms were oral literatures of Austronesian peoples—myths, legends, ritual songs, work chants—these voices passed down through generations form the maternal bloodline of Taiwanese literature.

**The Atayal's "Genesis" tells of the earth beginning from a single seed, the Bunun's "Eight-part Harmony" creates voices layered like mountain valleys, and the Paiwan's "Ancient Jade Mountain Legends" record the glorious epics of chiefs and warriors.** These works were not merely literature but carriers of ecological knowledge, philosophical contemplation, and social memory. The Amis's "Sea Festival Songs" were both homages to the Pacific and profound meditations on life-death cycles.

| Indigenous Group | Representative Works | Characteristics |
|------------------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Atayal | "Genesis" | Cosmic origin mythology |
| Bunun | "Eight-part Harmony" | Polyphonic chanting |
| Paiwan | "Ancient Jade Legend," "Children of the Sun" | Hierarchical society epics |
| Thao | "White Deer Maiden" | Love and nature |

These oral literatures embodied "**poetry is life, life is poetry**"—no division between creation and daily existence, each performance a rebirth of literature. More importantly, these ancient voices continue to resurrect in contemporary indigenous writers' works, becoming vital forces against cultural amnesia.

## The Dawn of Cross-Cultural Writing (1624–1683)

When the Dutch landed at Anping in Tainan in 1624, Taiwan formally entered recorded history. This period's greatest literary characteristic was "**cross-cultural writing**"—people from different cultural backgrounds using various languages to record their experiences in the new land.

Dutch missionaries used romanized scripts to record indigenous languages, creating Taiwan's earliest "bilingual literary" practices. As Han Chinese immigration increased, **Shen Guangwen (1612-1688), honored as "the founder of Taiwan's classical literature,"** pioneered Taiwan's Chinese-language literature with his "Wenkai Poetry Collection." During the Zheng period (1661-1683), Zheng Jing emphasized culture and education, further developing Chinese literature in Taiwan.

Works from this period bore the strong coloring of "immigrant literature"—both longing for homeland and curiosity about adapting to new territory. **In 1662, Shen Guangwen and Ji Qiguang, along with eleven others, founded the "Eastern Poetry Society," devoted to cultivating traditional Chinese literature and beginning Taiwan's poetry society tradition.**

## Literary Societies and Local Emergence (1683–1895)

During the Qing Dynasty's 200-year rule of Taiwan, Taiwanese literature gradually formed its distinctive characteristics. The most important literary phenomenon was the rise of "**poetry societies**"—beginning with the "Hsinchu Poetry Society" in 1752, societies like Tainan's "Haidong Academy Poetry Society" and Changhua's "Wenkai Poetry Society" sprouted like mushrooms after rain, creating the golden age of Taiwan's classical literature.

**A major characteristic of Qing-era Taiwanese literature was the prevalence of "bamboo branch poetry."** This poetic form originating from folk songs used colloquial language to depict Taiwan's customs and people, becoming an important window into ancient Taiwanese social life. The bamboo branch poems in Fan Xian's "Revised Gazetteer of Taiwan Prefecture" vividly portray the market life of Qing-era Taiwan.

Important writer groups included:
- **Cai Tinglan, a jinshi from Penghu** (representative work "Hainan Miscellany")
- **Poets from Changhua: Chen Zhaoxing and Huang Quan**
- **From Hsinchu: Zheng Yongxi and Lin Zhanmei**

By late Qing, Taiwanese intellectuals gradually awakened, recognizing that literature should not merely be a tool for entertainment and social interaction, but should reflect the suffering of local people. **During the Tongzhi and Guangxu reigns, the standard of Taiwanese poets' works was already equal to mainland China's**, with literary reputations spreading to the Chinese mainland. Famous local poets like Chen Weiying, Qiu Fengjia, and Shi Shihao produced works with strong local coloring and national consciousness.

## The Rise of New Literature Movement (1895–1945)

After Taiwan was ceded to Japan following the 1895 Sino-Japanese War, this historical upheaval brought revolutionary opportunities for Taiwanese literature. The Japanese colonial period was both the germination period of Taiwan's modern literature and the most revolutionary era in Taiwanese literary history.

### The New vs. Old Literature Debate and Vernacular Revolution

The 1920s saw Taiwan erupt in the intense "**New vs. Old Literature Debate**." The new literature faction, represented by Zhang Wojun, advocated abandoning classical Chinese for vernacular writing; traditionalists insisted on maintaining classical Chinese's status. This debate was not merely about literary form innovation but represented Taiwanese intellectuals' pursuit of modernity.

**Lai He (1894-1943) was revered as the "Father of Taiwan's New Literature."** His short story "Fighting for a Bustling Scene" (1926) was Taiwan's first vernacular short story, pioneering a new era for Taiwan's modern literature. Lai He's works possessed strong realist coloring and ethnic consciousness, exposing Japanese colonial rule's injustice through literature and calling for Taiwanese ethnic awakening.

> **📊 Data Source**
> According to the Lai He Literature and Education Foundation's statistics, Lai He composed over a thousand classical poems in his lifetime, plus vernacular works including fiction, new poetry, and essays in various genres, establishing the foundation of Taiwan's modern literature.

### Left-wing Literature and International Recognition

The 1930s, influenced by international left-wing thought, saw the emergence of Taiwanese writers with socialist coloring. **Yang Kui (1906-1985) was the representative figure; his short story "The Paperboy" was the first work by a Taiwanese writer to win awards in Japanese literary circles**, describing the miserable conditions of working-class laborers with strong class consciousness and resistance spirit.

### Cultural Resilience During Japanization

After the Pacific War erupted, Japan implemented the "Japanization Movement," restricting Taiwanese to publishing only in Japanese. **This period produced important writers like Lu Heruo, Zhang Wenhuan, and Weng Nao, who maintained concern for Taiwan's local culture within the framework of Japanese literature.**

Lu Heruo's "The Ox Cart" and Zhang Wenhuan's "The Capon," though written in Japanese, were deeply rooted in Taiwanese soil, demonstrating colonial writers' cultural resilience. **The three most important writers of the Japanese period were: Lai He, Yang Kui, and Wu Zhuoliu**, with Wu Zhuoliu writing "Orphan of Asia," "Fig," and "Taiwan Forsythia" from late Japanese rule through early postwar period, called the "Solitary Sail Trilogy," pioneering Taiwan's river novel tradition.

## Postwar Language Transition and Decline (1945–1960)

After Taiwan's retrocession in 1945, literary circles faced enormous language transition problems. Many Japanese-era Taiwanese writers had to relearn Chinese for creation, while mainland writers who came to Taiwan needed to adapt to Taiwan's regional environment. This "cross-linguistic" and "cross-strait" displacement became important characteristics of early postwar Taiwanese literature.

**More serious blows came from political persecution.** After the February 28 Incident, renowned writers like Lu Heruo, Zhang Wenhuan, Yang Kui, and Wang Baiyuan suffered varying degrees of political persecution, with half of the writers ceasing to write, sharply reducing Taiwanese literary works. Before the 1960s, narrowly defined Taiwanese literature showed stagnation, while what flourished was government-promoted anti-communist literature and nostalgic literature by mainlander writers.

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> Regarding the postwar Taiwanese literature "gap" problem, scholarly circles hold different interpretations. Some scholars attribute it to language policies, while others view it as natural evolution of literary tradition—this issue continues to be debated in academic circles.

## Parallel Development of Modernism and Nativist Realism (1960–1987)

The 1960s ushered in the golden age of modernism in Taiwanese literature. Following the Korean War's end and economic growth brought by American aid and American lifestyle, modernist literature emerged prominently. These works, including stream-of-consciousness novels, modern poetry, and absurdist literature, expressed dissatisfaction with formulaic anti-communist literature while harboring reformist consciousness against nostalgia in traditional culture.

### Pai Hsien-yung and the Peak of Modern Fiction

**Pai Hsien-yung's "Taipei People" was this period's most important short story collection.** Through exquisite prose and profound character portrayal, he depicted the spiritual world of mainlanders in Taiwan. Stories like "The Eternal Yin Xueyan" and "The Last Night of Madam Chin" not only achieved high technical standards but also profoundly reflected the impact of historical changes on individual destinies.

**Wang Wenxing's "Family Catastrophe" explored modern spiritual dilemmas through unique narrative techniques and linguistic experimentation, holding important status in literary history.** Writers like Qi Dengsheng and Chen Yingzhen also created new variants of modernist literature during this period.

### The 1970s Nativist Literature Debate

The 1970s saw Taiwan experience major political events like withdrawal from the UN and severed US-China diplomatic relations, with local consciousness strongly awakening. The famous "**Nativist Literature Debate**" erupted in 1977, with core issues being how Taiwanese literature should position itself and the relationship between literature and politics.

**Yu Guangzhong published "The Wolf is Coming" in the United Daily News, arguing that Taiwan's nativist literature "seemed to have secret correspondences" with mainland China's emphasis on class struggle in worker-peasant-soldier literature, specifically criticizing Chen Yingzhen, Wei Tiancong, and Wang Tuo.** After this article's publication, "for a time, the so-called 'blood-dropping blade' big hats made the literary world jumpy and filled with a murderous atmosphere."

**Huang Chunming was the flag-bearer of nativist literature, with works like "The Days of Watching the Sea" and "The Taste of Apples" using plain language to describe the fates of characters in Taiwan's villages and small towns.** Wang Zhenhe's "A Cartload of Dowry" used black humor to describe the absurdity and sorrow of Taiwan's countryside during modernization.

In 1964, **Wu Zhuoliu formally founded "Taiwan Literature," intending to promote the mission of the Japanese-era Taiwan Literary Arts Union and emphasizing Taiwan literature's inherent distinctiveness and autonomy.** This publication provided a platform for local writers to demonstrate their abilities, making realist aesthetic thinking highly esteemed.

## Post-Martial Law Diversity (1987–Present)

Taiwan lifted martial law in 1987, granting literary creation unprecedented freedom. This period saw Taiwanese literature develop in diversified directions, with various literary schools and styles coexisting in a flourishing scene.

**The same year, Yeh Shih-tao's "Outline of Taiwan Literary History" was published, establishing Taiwan literary history as a literary category and constructing its own history.** This work finally answered that harsh 1945 question—Taiwan not only has literature but possesses rich, deep literary traditions.

### Revival of Indigenous Literature

In the multicultural era's atmosphere, **indigenous literature began revival. Writers like Sun Dachuan, Walis Nokan, Syaman Rapongan, and Badai used modern literary forms to reinterpret indigenous cultural traditions and contemporary situations**, injecting new vitality into Taiwanese literature.

These writers were not "nostalgic for the past" but "redefining the present"—using contemporary literary techniques to reinterpret tribal wisdom and values, allowing indigenous voices to become mainstream in Taiwanese literature again after four centuries.

### New Generation Writers and Cross-boundary Creation

**New generation writers like Luo Yijun, Hu Shufen, Gan Yaoming, Wu Mingyi, Chen Xue, and Yi Geyan opened new possibilities for Taiwanese literature with fresh creative techniques and thematic concerns.** Their works often feature cross-cultural, cross-media characteristics, reflecting literary innovations of the globalization era.

**Writers like Wu Mingyi, Liu Kexiang, and Liao Hongji devoted themselves to nature writing, focusing on environmental issues**, demonstrating Taiwanese literature's contemplation of ecological crises. This "ecological literature" not only enriched Taiwanese literature's thematic scope but also embodied contemporary writers' sense of social responsibility.

### Mother Tongue Literature and Women's Writing

Another important postwar development was **the flourishing of mother tongue literature**, including Taiwanese-language literary creation by writers like Lin Zongyuan, Xiangyang, Lin Yangmin, and Huang Jinlian, plus Hakka literary creation by writers like Du Panfang, Zeng Guihai, and Huang Hengqiu.

**Women's literature became an important force in Taiwan's literary scene. Li Ang's "The Butcher's Wife" used bold themes and sharp writing to expose traditional patriarchal society's oppression of women.** Female writers like Liao Huiying ("Rape Seeds"), Xiao Sa, Su Weizhen, Xia Yu, and Yuan Qiongqiong represented "women's literature," while writers like Zhang Dachun and Huang Fan represented political literature and urban literature—all flourished during this period.

### New Literary Forms in the Digital Age

Entering the 21st century, Taiwanese literature faced dual challenges of globalization and digitization. **Internet literature emerged, with works like Jyzi Tsai's "First Intimate Contact" and Giddens' "Summer Poetry" demonstrating new literary forms in the new media era.** Science fiction, mystery, and fantasy genres also developed significantly, proving Taiwanese literature's diversity and innovation.

|--------|----------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Japanese Modernization | Lai He, Yang Kui | "Fighting Scene," "The Paperboy" | Anti-colonial, realism |
| Modernism | Pai Hsien-yung, Wang Wenxing | "Taipei People," "Family Catastrophe" | Modern techniques, urban experience |
| Nativist Realism | Huang Chunming, Wang Zhenhe | "Watching the Sea," "Cartload of Dowry" | Local concern, social criticism |
| Diverse Flowering | Indigenous writers, new generation | Coexistence of all ethnic groups and genres | Cross-cultural, environmental consciousness |

## Core Characteristics of Taiwanese Literature: Symphony of Multiple Voices

From indigenous ancestors' oral songs to contemporary writers' digital creation, Taiwan's literary history is an epic about voices seeking written form and words seeking homeland. Over four centuries, different voices have met, collided, and merged on this land, ultimately converging into an ever-flowing literary river.

**Taiwanese literature's characteristics lie not in singular style or theme, but in its inclusiveness and diversity.** It encompasses both the mysterious poetry of indigenous literature and the profound heritage of Chinese literature; both the modern sensibility of Japanese literature and the earthy fragrance of local literature. This symphony of multiple voices is Taiwanese literature's most precious treasure.

> **✦** "An island asked 'Does it have literature?' 30 years ago has today become one of the important centers of Chinese-language literary creation."

Looking toward the future, Taiwanese literature will continue advancing through multiple dialogues between tradition and modernity, local and international, ethnic groups and individuals. As poet Yu Guangzhong said: "Taiwan's most beautiful aspect is human feeling," and this human feeling flows eternally through the lines of Taiwanese literature.

**That harsh 1945 question "Does Taiwan have literature?" appears so pale before four centuries of literary history.** Taiwan not only has literature—Taiwanese literature continues writing stories belonging to this land and its people. From prehistoric oral epics to digital-age internet creation, each era adds new chapters to this epic.
