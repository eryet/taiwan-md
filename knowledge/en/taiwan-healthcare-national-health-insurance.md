# Taiwan's Healthcare and National Health Insurance

> **30-Second Overview:** On March 1, 1995, Taiwan's National Health Insurance (NHI) officially launched, overnight providing medical coverage to 4 million uninsured people. For 29 years, this system has maintained 99.9% population coverage and earned international acclaim as a miracle. But behind the glory, NHI faces a serious financial crisis—2024 deficit projections exceed NT$70 billion (US$2.2 billion). The system that has saved countless lives now needs saving itself.

At dawn on March 1, 1995, an elderly man arrived at Taichung Veterans General Hospital's emergency room with a heart attack. Previously, uninsured patients like him often faced "pay first, treat later" policies. But this night was different—he presented his newly issued NHI card and received treatment without worrying about crushing medical bills.

That day marked the official launch of Taiwan's National Health Insurance. Overnight, 4 million previously uninsured people (18% of the population) gained medical coverage. This system, dubbed the "Taiwan Miracle" by the international medical community, fundamentally changed how Taiwanese people access healthcare.

But 29 years later, this miracle faces unprecedented challenges.

## From Four Systems to Universal Coverage

The birth of NHI traces back to the chaotic state of Taiwan's medical insurance system in the early 1990s.

Taiwan then operated four separate medical insurance schemes: Government Employees Insurance (公保), Labor Insurance (勞保), Farmers Insurance (農保), and Military Personnel Insurance (軍保). This fragmented system created severe inequalities—Government Employees Insurance offered the best benefits, Labor Insurance came second, Farmers Insurance provided the least, while approximately 40% of the population (including students, housewives, and self-employed individuals) had no coverage at all.

A tragic example from that era: if a civil servant and a farmer contracted the same cancer, the civil servant enjoyed comprehensive benefits while the farmer might delay treatment due to unaffordable medical costs. This "healthcare based on status" system contradicted Taiwan's pursuit of social equality.

In 1988, the Department of Health began planning a universal health insurance system. The planning team included scholars like Tsai Hsun-hsiung, Wu Kai-hsun, Lan Chung-fu, Yang Chih-liang, and Chiang Tung-liang, who extensively studied medical insurance systems worldwide before designing Taiwan's version of universal healthcare.

This design featured several key characteristics: mandatory enrollment (preventing adverse selection), single payer (unified government administration), premium sharing (individuals, employers, and government sharing costs), and risk pooling (healthy individuals helping support those who are ill).

## The Miracle of Opening Day

March 1, 1995, marked a pivotal day in Taiwan's medical history.

The enrollment rate reached 92% on the first day, climbing to 96% within a year and currently maintaining stable coverage above 99.9%. This coverage rate ranks among the world's highest, surpassing even the United States (which still has approximately 8% uninsured population).

More remarkably, public satisfaction has remained consistently high. Since launch, NHI satisfaction rates have stayed between 80-90%, ranking among the top government policies. Even citizens with different political alignments view NHI positively.

Yang Chih-liang, who served as Department of Health Director during implementation, recalled: "NHI's implementation truly realized the ideal of 'healthcare accessible to all.' This was not just medical policy, but the practice of social justice."

## The Health Card Revolution: From Paper to IC Cards

Taiwan's NHI has also led the world in technological innovation.

Initially, health cards were paper-based with photographs that could be easily forged or misused. In 2004, Taiwan introduced NHI IC cards, becoming the world's first health insurance system to fully implement smart cards.

This small IC card revolutionized the healthcare experience: one card provided access to any medical facility across Taiwan; doctors could check patients' medication records to avoid duplicate prescriptions; during emergencies, even unconscious patients' allergy histories and critical medical information could be accessed.

The success of NHI IC cards prompted delegations from many countries to study Taiwan's experience. The United States referenced Taiwan's model when implementing the Affordable Care Act (Obamacare).

## The Unspoken Financial Crisis

However, beneath NHI's polished exterior lies a serious financial problem.

Since launch, NHI has operated like a family with expenses exceeding income. Medical costs continue rising while premium adjustments face political resistance. The premium rate was 4.25% at launch in 1995; by 2026, while the rate increased to 5.17%, it still cannot keep pace with growing medical expenses.

The data is stark: in 2024, NHI's projected deficit may exceed NT$70 billion (US$2.2 billion). Without reform, NHI's safety reserves could be depleted within years, facing bankruptcy.

Why has this happened? The reasons are complex:

**Population Aging**: Taiwan is entering a super-aged society, with elderly people requiring far more medical care than younger populations. People over 65 average four times the annual medical costs of those under 65.

**Expensive New Drugs**: Cancer targeted therapies and immunotherapy drugs cost hundreds of thousands of NT dollars per month, but the public expects NHI coverage.

**Medical Inflation**: Medical equipment and personnel costs continue rising, but NHI payment adjustments are always slow.

**System Abuse**: While not severe, problems exist including "medical tourism" by foreigners using NHI and unnecessary repeat visits.

## Drug Coverage Controversies: Life-Saving Medications vs. Financial Sustainability

The fiercest controversies often center on coverage for expensive medications.

In 2020, Spinraza, a drug treating Spinal Muscular Atrophy (SMA), entered Taiwan at approximately NT$600,000 (US$18,750) per dose requiring lifelong treatment. Patient families strongly advocated for NHI coverage, arguing economic capacity shouldn't determine life or death; but NHI Administration initially provided only conditional coverage considering financial burden.

Similar controversies recur regularly. Cancer patient groups frequently protest NHI's "overly strict gatekeeping," claiming it delays patient treatment; but health economists warn that uncontrolled costs could bankrupt NHI, ultimately harming all citizens.

This dilemma has no standard answer. After all, no insurance system can cover all medical needs—the key is finding balance between fairness and financial sustainability.

## DRG System: Hospitals' Love-Hate Relationship

To control medical costs, NHI Administration has promoted various reform measures, with Diagnosis Related Groups (DRGs) being the most controversial.

Simply put, DRGs provide hospitals "bundled pricing" by diagnosis type. For example, appendectomy surgery receives fixed NHI payment regardless of hospitalization length. This incentivizes hospital efficiency while avoiding unnecessary treatments.

In March 2016, Taiwan hospitals fully implemented DRG systems. Supporters believe this reduces medical waste and improves quality; critics worry hospitals might "cherry-pick patients" or "reduce services" to control costs.

An anonymous physician noted: "DRGs make us more cost-conscious, which is good. But facing complex cases, it's really difficult—should we treat comprehensively or appropriately limit intervention?"

## Tiered Healthcare: Ideals vs. Reality

Taiwan faces a unique problem: people are too accustomed to "directly attacking medical centers."

Citizens visit National Taiwan University Hospital for common colds or emergency rooms for minor scrapes. This "major hospital worship" wastes medical resources. The government has promoted "tiered healthcare" for years, hoping to establish a "clinics for minor illnesses, hospitals for major diseases" pattern, but with limited success.

Reasons are complex: insufficient public trust in clinics, inadequate co-payment differentials between hospitals and clinics, inconvenient referral systems. Most critically, NHI is too convenient—for just NT$150-420 (US$4.7-13) in co-payments, patients can see top specialists, naturally preferring to "directly see the best."

This phenomenon is rare internationally. In Britain, people must first see family doctors before specialist referrals; in America, uninsured emergency visits could cause bankruptcy. Taiwan's "medical equity" works too thoroughly, creating resource allocation distortions.

## Medical Tourism Controversies

Taiwan's NHI faces an unexpected challenge: "medical tourism."

Due to Taiwan's high medical quality and low costs, some foreigners specifically come for "medical tourism." More controversially, some overseas-residing Taiwanese nationals return to Taiwan to use NHI for medical care before leaving again.

This phenomenon raises fairness questions: is it fair for long-term premium payers and occasional users to enjoy equal treatment?

The government has begun policy adjustments, requiring overseas residents to have waiting periods when re-enrolling and raising thresholds for suspension and resumption of coverage. But implementation remains difficult, as verifying someone's residence status is not easy.

## The Pandemic Test

COVID-19 represented one of NHI's greatest challenges.

During early pandemic stages, the government quickly established "electronic fences" through the NHI system to track patient medical records; NHI Administration also urgently adjusted payment rules to support hospitals treating COVID patients. When international medical resources were scarce, Taiwan's NHI played a crucial role.

But the pandemic also increased NHI's financial burden. Massive testing, vaccine procurement, and isolation treatment costs all pressured NHI. Although the government allocated additional budgets, it highlighted NHI's vulnerability to sudden events.

## Reform Directions: Revenue Enhancement and Cost Control

Facing financial crisis, NHI reform is imperative.

**Revenue Enhancement**: The government is discussing premium rate adjustments, co-payment system reviews, and developing diverse income sources. But each involves political sensitivity, making progress difficult.

**Cost Control**: Promoting tiered healthcare, strengthening review mechanisms, developing preventive care, and establishing health technology assessment systems. These measures are relatively easier to implement but have limited effectiveness.

**System Innovation**: Some experts suggest learning from German experience to develop multiple-payer systems, or following Singapore's model combining savings accounts with insurance benefits. But these involve fundamental system reforms requiring social consensus.

## More Than Healthcare: A Social Contract

Taiwan's NHI significance extends far beyond medical insurance.

It embodies Taiwan society's core values: equality for all, mutual assistance, and caring for the vulnerable. It makes Taiwan one of the few countries achieving "healthcare human rights," where rich and poor enjoy equal medical services.

It also represents an important outcome of Taiwan's democratization. Under authoritarian rule, healthcare was privileged-class exclusive; after democratization, healthcare became a universal right. High NHI satisfaction reflects public support for this system.

But current challenges are unprecedented. Population aging, advancing medical technology, and rising public expectations all pressure NHI. How to maintain fairness while ensuring financial sustainability is a challenge Taiwan society must collectively address.

## NHI's Future

29 years ago, Taiwan created the NHI miracle. 29 years later, this miracle needs new innovations to continue.

Whether through technological innovation (AI-assisted diagnosis, telemedicine), institutional innovation (value-based payments, preventive care enhancement), or social innovation (medical resource sharing, community care integration), Taiwan must continue exploring.

Most importantly, Taiwan society needs to reconsider NHI's positioning. It's not just "insurance" but a "social contract"—everyone has obligations to pay premiums and rights to enjoy healthcare; everyone must take responsibility for system sustainability while benefiting from it.

In the global wave of healthcare reform, Taiwan's NHI remains a model worth learning from. It proves that even with limited resources, through institutional design and social solidarity, healthcare justice ideals can be achieved.

As long as Taiwan people continue believing in this ideal, NHI will continue creating miracles.
